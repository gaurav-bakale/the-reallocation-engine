**What you'll be able to do:** Generate application materials whose visa framing is calibrated to what each employer tier actually understands about OPT — strategic, accurate, and never misrepresenting your authorization.

## Learning outcomes

- **(Create)** Generate tier-appropriate framing for a real role.
- **(Evaluate)** Choose what to disclose, and when, by sponsorship tier.
- **(Understand)** Explain the FICA-exemption employer benefit accurately.

## Opening case — the rejection that wasn't about you

A strong candidate, authorized to work, applies to a role they could do well. In the first screen, the recruiter sees "international student" and a set of assumptions fires before a single question is asked: *this means sponsorship, this means cost, this means legal risk, this means a delay we don't have time for.* The candidate is filtered out — not for their ability, and not for anything true. Because in the OPT and STEM-OPT window, **none of those assumptions hold**: the candidate is already authorized to work, needs no immediate sponsorship, and actually saves the employer money. The candidate lost the role not to a competitor but to a misunderstanding they did nothing to correct.

Most international candidates don't know how to correct it. Many don't know there's anything to correct. This chapter is about saying the true thing, well, to the right audience, at the right time.

## What you need first

From Chapter 9, an "Apply" recommendation and the company's sponsorship tier — because framing is *tier-dependent*. From Chapter 3, the contract, in its sharpest moral form here: framing presents accurate information strategically; it never invents it. Cross that line and the tool becomes the thing the book exists to prevent.

## The component this chapter rests on

The **OPT Framing Generator**, the engine's fourth component. It produces application language — the authorization sentence in a cover letter, the way work eligibility appears on a résumé or in a screen — matched to what a given employer tier is likely to understand. Its governing constraint is a hard rule, stated before any feature: **framing is accurate information presented strategically — never fabricated credentials, invented metrics, or misrepresented status.**

## Core content — the FICA fact, and framing by tier

First, the fact most candidates can state but few deploy. Students on F-1 status working under OPT are generally **exempt from FICA taxes** (Social Security and Medicare) — which means the employer is also exempt from the matching contribution, roughly **7.65%** of wages, for the OPT period.[^fica] Hiring an OPT candidate is, for that window, *cheaper* than hiring an equivalent domestic worker, not more expensive. That is a real, accurate, employer-facing benefit — and it is the opposite of the assumption in the opening case.

Now the framing, calibrated by the tier from Chapter 5:

- **Proven** (the company sponsors and knows the terrain): **state authorization directly.** They understand OPT; clarity is an asset; no need to soft-pedal.
- **Likely** (some sponsorship history, may not be fluent in the details): **lead with work authorization and the FICA benefit; avoid leading with the acronym.** Say "authorized to work in the U.S. and FICA-exempt for the coming period," not a jargon string that triggers the opening case's assumptions.
- **Unknown** (no evidence either way): **no visa mention in the initial written materials.** The first screen is the wrong place to raise a topic the employer may misread; framing moves to the interview, where you can answer questions in context rather than seed them on paper.
- **Avoid:** there are no materials — the engine already said skip (Chapter 9). Don't write framing for a role you're not applying to.

The logic of the ladder is one idea: **disclose in proportion to the audience's understanding.** Where the employer understands OPT, be direct. Where they half-understand, lead with the benefit and the authorization. Where they don't understand and have shown no signal, don't hand them a misunderstanding in writing — let it surface in conversation where you can shape it.

## A decision-rule table (anatomy item 10)

| Tier | Visa mention in written materials? | Lead with | Hard rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| Proven | Yes, direct | "Authorized; I/we know the OPT→H-1B path" | Never overstate the timeline you can offer |
| Likely | Yes, framed | "Authorized to work + FICA-exempt (~7.65% saving)" | Never imply you need no future sponsorship if you will |
| Unknown | No (initial) | Your fit; visa handled in interview | Never conceal authorization if directly asked |
| Avoid | — | (no materials) | — |

The hard rule cuts both ways: you never *over*-claim (e.g., implying permanent authorization you don't have) and you never *deny or hide* status when asked directly. Strategic timing of true information is fine. A false impression is not.

## Worked example — one candidate, three framings

**Situation.** The same graduate, the same data role, imagined at three companies in three tiers.

- **Proven biotech:** cover letter states plainly: "I'm authorized to work in the U.S. on STEM OPT and familiar with the H-1B timeline." Clear, confident, correct.
- **Likely startup:** "I'm authorized to work in the U.S. and, as an OPT employee, I'm FICA-exempt — a ~7.65% payroll saving for the coming period." Leads with benefit and authorization; the acronym is explained, not dropped as a flag.
- **Unknown firm:** the written application says nothing about visa; it sells fit. In the interview, when authorization comes up, the candidate explains OPT, the FICA benefit, and the timeline in context.

**The decision.** Same true facts, three presentations, each matched to what the audience can hear.

**The lesson (one sentence):** Disclosure is a function of audience understanding, not a fixed script — and never a function of what you wish were true.

**The limit (where this fails):** Tiers are probabilistic (Chapter 5), so the framing can be miscalibrated — an "Unknown" may actually be sophisticated, a "Likely" may be confused. And framing cannot fix a genuine timeline problem (Chapter 8) or substitute for sponsorship a company won't offer. It optimizes presentation, not reality. This is also the chapter most in need of a legal/ethics reviewer before publication — the disclosure rules touch employment and immigration law.

## Mid-chapter checkpoint

The line you must feel, not just know: **"avoid leading with the acronym" is strategy; "say you're a citizen" is fraud.** If a framing ever requires you to state something untrue, or to deny your status when directly asked, the generator has failed and you stop. Strategic emphasis and timing of accurate facts is the entire toolkit. The moment it shades into a false impression, you've left this book's method.

## What the machine could not know

The generator knows a company's tier and the general OPT facts. It cannot know that this specific recruiter was themselves an international student and will respond best to total directness regardless of tier. It cannot know that a particular "Unknown" company was burned by a visa timeline last year and will ask about authorization in the first email no matter what your materials say. It cannot make the ethical call for you in a gray moment. Framing is a starting calibration; reading the actual human across the table — and holding the honesty line when it's tempting not to — is irreducibly yours.

## Key terms

- **Framing:** presenting accurate information strategically, calibrated to the audience's understanding — never fabrication.
- **FICA exemption:** OPT employees are generally exempt from Social Security/Medicare tax, saving the employer ~7.65% for the period — a real hiring benefit.
