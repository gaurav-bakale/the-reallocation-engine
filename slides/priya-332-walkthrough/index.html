Priya’s First 80 Days

How one new grad runs a 3-3-2 search instead of spray-and-pray — step by step

A persona walkthrough. Priya is composite, not a real person. Every step maps to a chapter and a runnable recipe.
Act 1 · The trap

Meet Priya. She just graduated from Northeastern.

MS, Information Systems. F-1 visa. One deployed class project. No job yet — and the OPT clock is about to start.
Set the human stakes before any tool appears. She is capable; the problem is allocation, not effort.
Act 1 · The trap

The clock starts the day her OPT does.

Ninety cumulative unemployment days. Weekends count.
On post-completion OPT, 90 aggregate days of unemployment ends the record — no grace period. The engine budgets to 80.
Act 1 · The trap

Her instinct: apply to everything.

Spray-and-pray: volume in, silence out.
Cold applications convert near 0.2%. Eight hours a day of applying feels productive and moves almost nothing.
Act 1 · The trap

The counter goes up. The search does not move.

Two weeks in: 200 applications, 3 auto-rejections, 0 conversations. The clock has spent 14 of 90 days.
The trap is fluency of effort: it looks like progress. Feedback (the counter) is not the same as return.
Act 1 · The trap

Spend effort where the return is, not where the feedback is.

That one sentence is the reallocation principle. The rest is making it achievable.
The reallocation principle.
This is the hinge of the whole deck. Everything after operationalizes it.
Act 2 · The 3-3-2 plan

The 3-3-2 day: where the hires actually come from.

2 hours applying · 3 networking · 3 portfolio.
Most 2025 hires came through connections (~54%); validated skills carried a ~56% wage premium. The day follows the return.
Act 2 · The 3-3-2 plan

Two hours of applying is a forcing function for quality.

If you can only apply for two hours, you apply to fewer roles — so you must filter hard first. The filter is where the signal lives.
Not 'apply less' as an end. Fewer, better applications, freeing six hours for the channels that pay.
Act 2 · The 3-3-2 plan

Same hours, different outcomes.

Spray wins only on raw count. 3-3-2 wins on everything that moves a search.
Illustrative, per Ch.2: ~25 targeted apps, 12 outreach → 4 informationals → 2 referrals, 1 deployed project. Fewer applications, more movement.
Act 2 · The 3-3-2 plan

A freed hour follows a rule, not a mood.

The deviation rule keeps 3-3-2 from becoming a ritual.
Judgment, not autopilot. The tool handles the filter; Priya decides where the marginal hour goes.
Act 3 · Build the filter

To make two hours beat eight, Priya builds a filter.

Set up in ten minutes.
She clones the engine, reads the map, and checks what actually runs before trusting anything.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 1 — find who just raised money.

Recent raises beat big names.
Funded companies are hiring. She starts from the public record, not a brand list.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 2 — keep only employers who actually sponsor.

Sponsorship history, not fame.
A household name with no sponsorship record is a dead end for her visa. The scorer separates record from reputation.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 3 — drop postings that are not real.

Detection is not liveness.
Half of 'open' roles are ghosts. She keeps only postings proven live today.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 4 — check the role is worth it.

Proposing is not confirming.
The title resolves to a real occupation with real pay before she trusts it.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 5 — zero anything that cannot close in time.

The visa timeline is a gate, not a vote.
A role that cannot realistically close before the cliff is skipped, however attractive.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 6 — one defensible score: Apply / Consider / Skip.

Equal fit, different sponsorship, opposite calls.
The composite is traceable: she can say why each role landed where it did.
Act 3 · Build the filter

The funnel, run for real: 300 down to the few worth two hours.

The filter turns volume into a shortlist.
Now her two applying-hours hit 25 pre-checked roles instead of spraying 200.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 7 — frame the visa truthfully, and strategically.

Strategic framing, never misrepresentation.
She states OPT plainly when asked; she leads with the relevant true fact. The tool lives on one side of the line.
Act 3 · Build the filter

Step 8 — a resume that survives the filter and the human.

Survive the parser without becoming keyword soup.
Both readers must pass it: the filter that screens, the person who decides.
Act 4 · Run the week

Run the recipes; every stage leaves a trail.

The chain is only as trustworthy as its weakest link.
She runs the engine end to end; the RUN_LOG records what each step found.
Act 4 · Run the week

Watch the skip rate. Skipping is the point.

A low skip rate means the filter is failing.
57% skipped this week — healthy. The engine is earning its keep by talking her out of bad applications.
Act 4 · Run the week

What the machine cannot know stays Priya’s call.

It cannot know the hiring manager is her professor’s co-founder, or which mission she can speak about with conviction. The score informs; she decides.
Guard against both failure modes: ignoring the filter, and obeying it as ritual. Judgment is what she brings.
Act 5 · Outcome

Thirty days in: movement, with margin on the clock.

Same 30 days. Far more of the things that become offers.
Not a guarantee — a better-allocated search. The clock spent the same; the pipeline looks nothing alike.
Act 5 · Outcome

The clock, 30 days in.

Fifty days of margin, and a pipeline that is moving.
Reallocation bought her real conversations while the counter was still young.
Act 5 · Outcome

Spend effort where the return is.

Machines verify conformance; humans verify adequacy.
